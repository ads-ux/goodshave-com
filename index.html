Let's be honest: most men learned to shave from watching their dad, and most dads weren't exactly shaving experts. The result? Decades of razor burn, ingrown hairs, and overpaying for mediocre gear — and that's before you even get to trimmers, cream, and aftershave.

How we tested: Over 90+ days, our team evaluated 31 products across five categories — razors, electric shavers, beard trimmers, shaving cream/soap, and aftershave — across four skin types (normal, sensitive, coarse, and acne-prone). We tracked performance, irritation levels, longevity, and per-use cost. Rankings reflect those results, not commission rates.

Whether you want the closest possible shave, the lowest-maintenance electric routine, or the right aftercare to stop the burn, we have a tested pick for you below.

Pro Tips

5 Things That Matter More Than Your Razor

Most shaving problems aren't about the hardware
🧊

Warm water prep

30 seconds of warm water softens the hair shaft by up to 70%. Always shower before shaving or use a warm towel.

🧴

Use real shave cream

Canned foam strips moisture. A proper shave cream or soap provides far better lubrication and less drag.

↔️

Shave with the grain first

First pass always goes with the grain. Against-the-grain first passes cause 80% of ingrown hairs.

📅

Replace blades sooner

Dull blades require more pressure = more irritation. Replace after 5–7 shaves, not 10+ as brands suggest.

❄️

Cold water rinse after

Cold water closes pores and reduces inflammation. Skip the hot rinse to finish.

🧪

Alcohol-free aftershave

Alcohol stings and dries the skin. Use a balm with witch hazel or aloe vera instead.

FAQ

Common Questions

The most common shaving questions, answered directly
Why don't you cover subscription razor services? +
We deliberately keep this roundup to razors and products you can buy directly, one time, from a retailer — no subscriptions, no recurring billing you have to remember to cancel. It also means every pick here links to the same place (Amazon), so you can compare prices and reviews yourself before buying instead of taking a subscription company's word for it.
How often should you replace razor blades? +
Replace cartridge razor blades after every 5–7 shaves, not the 10+ that brands suggest. A dull blade requires more pressure to cut, which is the primary cause of razor burn and ingrown hairs. For double-edge safety razors like the Merkur 34C, replace the blade after 3–5 shaves since they're sharper and can feel more aggressive as they dull.
What is the best razor for sensitive skin and razor bumps? +
The Gillette SkinGuard is the best cartridge razor for sensitive skin. Its fin guard reduces direct blade-to-skin contact, dramatically lowering irritation for men with coarse hair or razor bump-prone skin. For an alternative approach, a double-edge safety razor like the Merkur 34C — once technique is mastered — actually causes less irritation than multi-blade cartridges because a single sharp blade cuts cleanly rather than tugging repeatedly.
Is switching to a safety razor worth it? +
Yes, for most men who stick with it. The Merkur 34C costs around $40–50 upfront, but replacement blades cost $0.10–0.30 each — a 100-pack of Astra blades runs about $11. After the handle pays itself off (usually 3–6 months), you'll spend almost nothing on blades. The learning curve is real — expect 2–3 weeks to develop proper angle and pressure — but the shave quality and long-term savings are both excellent.
What causes razor burn and ingrown hairs? +
The four main causes are: (1) dull blades requiring excessive pressure, (2) shaving against the grain on the first pass, (3) insufficient prep — skipping warm water and proper shave cream, and (4) using alcohol-based aftershave that dries and inflames the skin. Fix these four things and most shaving problems disappear regardless of which razor you use.
Is an electric razor or a manual razor better? +
It depends on what you're optimizing for. A manual razor like the Merkur 34C or Gillette SkinGuard gives a closer shave and better results for sensitive or bump-prone skin, but takes longer and requires water/cream. An electric shaver like the Braun Series 9 Pro+ is faster, works dry, and travels well, but won't get quite as close. Most men who want the closest possible shave for a special occasion reach for a manual razor; most men who shave every day and value speed reach for electric.
How often should you replace a beard trimmer's blades? +
Trimmer blades hold up far longer than razor blades — most last 12–18 months of regular use before they start tugging instead of cutting cleanly. Signs it's time to replace: pulling or snagging hairs, uneven length results, or visible nicks on the blade teeth. Cleaning and oiling the blades after every use (a step most men skip) roughly doubles their working life.
What's the difference between shaving cream and shaving soap? +
Shaving cream (like GilletteLabs Rapid Foaming Gel or Cremo) is pre-lathered or lathers quickly with just your hands, making it faster for everyday use. Shaving soap needs a brush and bowl to work up a lather, which takes longer but typically produces a thicker, longer-lasting cushion that many wet-shaving enthusiasts prefer for reducing irritation with safety razors. For daily convenience, choose cream. For the closest, most cushioned traditional shave, choose soap and a brush.
Should aftershave contain alcohol? +
For most men, no. Alcohol-based aftershaves sting on freshly shaved skin and dry it out, which can actually worsen razor burn over time. Alcohol-free balms like Proraso Sensitive Skin or Brickell's Instant Relief soothe and hydrate instead. The exception is men with naturally oily skin who like alcohol's astringent, quick-drying feel — but even then, a lower-alcohol or witch-hazel-based formula is gentler than a traditional splash.
