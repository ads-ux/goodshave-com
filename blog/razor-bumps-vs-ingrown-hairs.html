Razor Bumps vs. Ingrown Hairs: What's the Difference (and How to Stop Both)

They look similar and get lumped together constantly — but they're not quite the same thing, and knowing the difference changes how you should treat them.

By the GoodShave editorial team · Updated July 2026 · 6 min read

If you've ever finished shaving and found your neck covered in small, irritated bumps, you've probably called it "razor bumps" or "ingrown hairs" interchangeably. They're closely related, but not identical — and the distinction actually matters for how you fix it.

What's the Actual Difference?

Ingrown hair is the broad term: any hair — shaved, waxed, or tweezed — that curls back or grows sideways into the skin as it regrows, triggering a small, localized inflammatory bump.

Pseudofolliculitis barbae (PFB), or "razor bumps," is the more specific, chronic version tied directly to shaving. A razor cuts the hair to a sharp point below or right at the skin's surface. As it regrows, that sharp tip re-penetrates the follicle wall or nearby skin, triggering a foreign-body immune response — and unlike a one-off ingrown hair, PFB tends to recur in the same spots shave after shave, sometimes leading to scarring or dark spots (post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation) if it isn't managed.

Both conditions share the same root cause: hair that curves sharply as it grows back tends to double back into the skin instead of exiting cleanly.

Who's Most at Risk

Curly, coarse, or thick facial hair is the single biggest risk factor for both conditions, because curved hair follicles produce hair that's naturally more likely to curl back on itself. PFB disproportionately affects Black men — some studies estimate 45–85% experience it at some point — and it's also common among Hispanic men and anyone else with naturally curly facial hair. Shaving very close and very frequently increases risk for everyone, regardless of hair type.

Prevention That's Actually Backed by Evidence

  • Shave with the direction of hair growth, not against it — this is the single most-cited prevention step across dermatology sources.
  • Replace blades regularly (roughly every 5–7 shaves) — dull blades increase tugging and uneven cuts that make curling more likely.
  • Exfoliate regularly — a washcloth in circular motion, or a chemical exfoliant like salicylic acid or glycolic acid, helps keep follicle openings clear so regrowing hair can exit normally.
  • Warm compress before shaving, cool compress after — heat softens hair and opens follicles; cold calms inflammation afterward.

When to See a Dermatologist

If bumps persist despite changing your technique, show signs of infection (increasing pain, pus, spreading redness), or start leaving scars or keloids, it's time to see a dermatologist rather than keep adjusting your routine on your own. A dermatologist can also rule out look-alike conditions like fungal folliculitis, which needs different treatment entirely.

This article is general education, not medical advice. If you're dealing with persistent or severe razor bumps, ingrown hairs, or skin irritation, please consult a dermatologist.
